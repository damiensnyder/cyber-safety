Facial Recognition: Background Information

Written by Nels Schimek. Published 03/12/2020.

To create facial recognition technology, a neural network is trained on a huge amount of facial data to recognize specific features, and forms a specific identification of a person based on the features. Below is a simplified example of how a neural network is designed, going from an image of a face to an identification based on the face.

The structure of an artificial neural network (ANN).

A database of known faces is created (driver’s licence, mugshots) for the government website or private company. When used live on people, the neural network measures the distinguishable features and compares these to known database to look for identification matches.

Facial features identified by an ANN.

There are certainly security benefits from the technology. Using biometric authentication increases one’s personal security, as it is much harder for someone to spoof or fake facial data than other forms of identification. It makes identity verification much easier and safer, as there are not keys or IDs that can be stolen, reducing cases of fraud or stolen identity. It would also aid in airport security, and aid law enforcement security in their ability to find criminals or suspects after a crime.

However, there are also significant negative issues that come from this technology. Because this technology is often implemented in real time, there is the issue of decreased personal privacy when people are in public, as they won’t always know if their face is being scanned. This issue is even more of a problem considering that most governments are still working on policy related to real time facial recognition surveillance, so what is acceptable and what is not allowed has not really been defined, giving rise to the issue of public surveillance without just cause. As a result of all this, there is also a significant lack of public knowledge or consent with how this technology and the data is collected will be used. In terms of the technology, another issue is that to get enough data to train the neural network, systems end up being trained on mostly white male data, leading to an increased likelihood for women or people of color to be misidentified. The technology struggles to differentiate between twins, and as problems with misidentification if someone has aged relative to their comparison image, or if the image quality is poor, due to factors such as glare, blurring, or far distances. Finally, there is the issue of secure data storage, as facial information could become a target for malicious actors.

Encryption can protect your data from attackers.

It is useful to protect your data not only from large collections used in AI but also from malicious actors. There are a number of small actions that individuals can take to make themselves less vulnerable. For password security, the best thing you can do is to use a different password for each different site. Remembering all of these can be a tall task, so using a service like LastPass, which securely stores all your passwords and matches them to the appropriate site, can make this process easier and safer. If sites offer 2-factor authentication or biometric authentication, those measures are recommended. While it may take longer to log in, it is significantly more secure. Be cognizant when opening URLs. A secure site will have “https://” at the start. A site without this may be insecure, and some sites may try and trick users by starting with “http://”, removing the “s” to try and get people to click on an insecure site without knowing it. As well, it is good practice to never open links or files from unknown sources. For your devices, update whenever possible. While it may be annoying, updates to security are constantly being rolled out. Make sure reputable anti-malware software is downloaded and updated. It is also a good idea to keep your files backed up in multiple locations, using tools such as Google Drive or Dropbox. In case of an information breach, if anything happens to your device or you need to shut it down, you are much less likely to lose your information.

Case Study: United States

Written by Fernanda Oropeza. Published 03/12/2020.

After Maryland granted license to people without asking for confirmation or proof of citizenship, ICE is using AI technology to run scans on license plates to match that with undocumented immigrants. Maryland became the first state on the east coast to do so and has issued over 275,000 licenses since 2013. This use of license search is not new, as other states have used such technology but the problem is that ICE officials in Maryland are doing so without outside approval (e.g. warrants).

ICE now runs license plate scans to find undocumented immigrants.

The use of this targets one of the most vulnerable populations within the United States, and there are no regulations to how it can be used. It can be seen as a breach of citizens' privacy. It also has different implications on different sets of people but ultimately is an example of using AI technology without taking into account ethics or morality.

AI is being used by different groups of people. Relevant examples include police departments and ICE, as well provide the implications of such uses on the privacy of the general population within the United States. The information data was also taken hold of by hackers recently which poses a new problem and possible threat. A problem with Clearview AI is also the fact that while it has helped police departments, there are a ton of issues as well that provide concerns to the general public. These include things such as racial profiling and the fact that the AI technology often has issues when confronted with images of people of color as it tends to be inaccurate.

This is a new age of technology and with it comes a whole slew of questions regarding ethics and morality when using them. Also questions on rules and regulations are in a grey area since there are varying perspectives on how to even begin drafting regulations in the first place. There is a lot of fear of breach on public anonymity and security as well.

Case Study: Hong Kong

Written by Tiffany Pham. Published 03/12/2020.

In June of 2019, China proposed an extradition bill that would allow people to be extradited over to mainland China. Believing that the bill would enable unjust processing and seizure of political dissidents in Hong Kong, demonstrations began, and they continue to escalate to this current day. The Hong Kong Protests provide a real life look at how cybersecurity technology can be used to abet injustice and privacy violations against citizens, creating a cycle of aggression and cyberwarfare.

Large protest in the streets of Hong Kong.

Hong Kong’s Legislative Council denies the use of automated facial recognition in their security cameras:

“According to information provided by government departments, no government department has procured or developed AFR-CCTV systems or applied AFR technology in CCTV systems.” —Secretary Patrick Nip

However, cybersecurity technology is present in many other ways. Hong Kong’s government currently makes use of:

  • 39,071 CCTV Cameras
  • An unspecified number of “temporary” CCTV cameras
  • 50 smart lamps (capable of “industrial grade computer for artificial intelligence image processing” and containing a “Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) tag”)

These devices were all placed with the intent of maintaining public security and preventing crime, however, in light of the protests, cybersecurity tech such as the above have turned into weapons rather than shields. Arrests have been made based on people’s online activity, gathered by police through cybersecurity tech, such as in the case of Colin Cheung, featured in the New York Times. In addition, spyware apps aimed at pro-democracy protesters are highly suspected to be posing as legitimate apps and have been downloaded by many, putting them at risk of privacy breaches and information theft.

Lamppost taken down by Hong Kong demonstrators.

On both sides, new security technology and cybersecurity tactics are being used to aid and abet violence, whether it be against protestors through the use of identifying tech that allows for police brutality to happen discreetly, to the doxxing (malicious publication of personal identifying information) of law enforcement officers. In all, there have been 7,300 total arrests, with most of those arrested being adults ranging from 21 to 30 years old. Among those 7,300 people, 105 of them are children aged between 12 and 15 years old.

The ongoing Hong Kong Protests prove that the advancement of AI technology, if left unchecked, can create human rights issues once an organization or government decides to weaponize technology against the common people. It is more than just an individual concern. It is worldwide. Read more on the protests here.

Activist Poetic: Tammy Lai-Ming Ho

Written by Fernanda Oropeza. Published MM/DD/YYYY.

Tammy Lai-ming Ho.

Poetry shows the scale of the issue through a single human’s eyes. Below is one titled “We Are What We Are Made Of” (July 2019) by Tammy Lai-Ming Ho, an associate professor at Hong Kong Baptist University and president of PEN Hong Kong. Her poetry on the Hong Kong Protests demonstrates a reality for many.

The poem is lifted from the Los Angeles Review of Books blog post by Andrea Lingenfelter. Inside the poem itself one can find explicit references to the start of the protests (June 2019) and the everyday imagery found among the streets of Hong Kong (walls "covered in squared colours"). While the case study presents the protests from an outside perspective focused on the potential abuses of security technology, the poem shows the will of actual protesters and their supporters.

Beginning from every day

tears are shed: their tails are puffs

of smoke. Beginning from yesterday

walls are covered in squared colours,

street names changed. Beginning from then

poetry can mean, be, and stay. Beginning

from June 2019, people

in a city look at each other:

million faces, million thoughts,

united in water, practice, slogans.

Beginning from now,

there is no turning back, no stopping.

We are what we are made of:

desperation and unbeatable will.

This is the beginning of the open

secret that we don’t ever quit.
